## 1. Information starts with bits

A classical computer stores information in bits. One bit has one definite value: either `0` or `1`.

Think of a light switch. Off can represent `0`, and on can represent `1`. A true-or-false question is similar because its answer is one of two choices.

## 2. Probability is a repeated pattern, not one result

One random result does not reveal much. If a coin lands heads once, we cannot conclude that it always lands heads.

Repeated trials make a pattern easier to see. A fair coin tossed 10 times does not have to give exactly 5 heads. After 1000 tosses, however, the ratio will often be much closer to one half.

![Repeated trials](assets/images/repeated_trials.svg)

## 3. A formula can state probability briefly

The probability of getting `1` can be written as:

$$
P(1)=\frac{1}{2}
$$

Skip the formula if it feels unfamiliar. It does not mean that every short experiment splits perfectly in half. It means that the ratio of `1` tends to approach one half after many trials under the same conditions.

## 4. Quantum measurement also produces probability patterns

When a qubit is measured, the visible result is still `0` or `1`. That is why it helps to understand repeated probability experiments before studying quantum information.

The simulation in this level is a classical warm-up, not yet a quantum experiment. Run it several times and compare how a short experiment moves around while a long experiment becomes more stable.
